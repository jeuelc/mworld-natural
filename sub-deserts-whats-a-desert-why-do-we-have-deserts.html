Why Do We Have Deserts?

If you live near the sea, it probably rains quite often. But far inland, clouds and rain rarely arrive, and deserts form. This is called the 'continentality' effect, because the dryness affects the interior of large continents. Cold oceans nearby make this worse, because they release less rain than warm oceans.

Another way in which deserts form is when high mountains block the path of clouds and rain. Rain mostly falls on the side of the mountains that face the ocean. A 'rainshadow' desert can form on the other side. Many deserts are in places where the overturning of the atmosphere makes air pressure high. High pressure causes clear, sunny skies, and there is little chance of rain.

Do people also cause deserts? Possibly. Though mostly related to low rainfall, desert areas can be made harsher and perhaps larger when too many animals graze there. Gathering too much firewood for cooking can also reduce the plant cover, and may also contribute to the expansion of desert-like conditions.
