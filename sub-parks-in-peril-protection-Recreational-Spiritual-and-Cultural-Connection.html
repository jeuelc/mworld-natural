Have you visited a park to find medicine, food or fuel? Maybe you went there to see interesting birds or plants, mountains or coastlines, to see a historical landmark or just to hike and camp. We visit parks for lots of reasons, and all of them are important and valuable.

Experiencing nature and visiting natural environments can even be good for your health and well-being, particularly if you live in a town or city.

Sometimes great artists travel to forests or mountains to find inspiration for their next masterpiece.

Parks provide an amazing array of benefits to humans: Their beautiful scenery can be like an art gallery, museum or even cathedral to some. Seeing native wildlife gives us a zoo like no other, and the chance to learn about or study natural areas. Finally, they can provide us with services and resources. We call this 'Ecological Capital'.

Some parks are home to people they depend on the protected area for food, firewood and land for grazing. These people often have a deep cultural connection to the land they live on.

But parks are fragile, and too many visitors can be damaging. You need to make sure never to pick native plants when you are in a park, and to take out anything that you bring in.

Ecological Capital

Ecological capital, or natural capital, is the way that we put an economic value on resources or services that we get from the natural world.
