The water that often moves through the rocks beneath deserts comes from wetter areas outside the desert. In those wetter areas, the water soaks into the ground, to begin its long journey. As it seeps along, it can collect materials from the rocks through which it passes.

Precious metals like gold and uranium can be gathered up in this way, slowly travelling underground in the water. These materials dissolve in the water partly because, deep underground, the water can become quite hot from the heat escaping from the interior of the Earth. At special locations beneath the desert, the metals are set down and left behind, as the water keeps moving.

Over long periods of time, this slow process forms rich ore deposits that can be mined. The deserts in Australia have the biggest deposits of uranium in the world. Ore bodies that formed in this way, laid down from warm underground water, are called 'hydrothermal' deposits.
