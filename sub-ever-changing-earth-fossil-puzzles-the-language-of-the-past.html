The Language of The Past

Plants and animals have evolved to suit the environment around them. Because of this, looking at the fossils in a rock gives you clues about the environment the rock formed in.

'Marine', or ocean-dwelling, creatures such as shellfish are good signs that the sedimentary rock formed in the ocean. In comparison, dinosaurs, trees and other land-loving organisms indicate environments above sea-level.

The size of the 'growth rings' you can see in fossil wood tell you the rate at which the tree grew. Because plants need light to grow, these rings reveal whether the tree was basking in the sun or had limited light.

Seawater is rich in carbon and oxygen, two elements that sea animals use to breathe and build their shells. But these elements change with temperature - so the chemistry of fossil shells can tell you how hot or cold the seawater was when the animal was living. Remember: fossils are like puzzle pieces - the more we have, the more complete our picture will be!
