Never-ending sediments

Imagine a really slow hourglass that poured one millimetre of sand every year. This is about the rate that many environments collect sediment. If you are 10 years old, then the amount of sand that would have piled up in your lifetime would only be about the width of your finger! But how thick would the sand be after one million years? One kilometre thick!

But the Earth is many, many millions of years old so, how can the Earth hold so much sediment?

The fact is it can't! When sediments have filled in a low area on the Earth's surface (called a 'basin') like a river valley, sea or lake, the sediments can get washed away. This is called 'erosion', and results in large gaps in our geological history of the Earth. The Earth can also sag under the weight of sediment. The Earth may seem hard and solid... it sure feels like it when you trip over! But with kilometres of sediment, and lots of time, even the Earth's surface sags, or 'subsides', under the weight.
