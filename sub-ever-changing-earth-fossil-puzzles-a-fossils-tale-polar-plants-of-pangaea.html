Polar plants of Pangaea

'Glossopteris' is a type of fossil leaf found in rocks from the 'Permian Period' on the geological time scale. That was from about 252 - 290 million years ago. This leaf fossil was first found in India and Australia during the early 1800s. Soon after, however, these fossils were found all around the Southern Hemisphere, including Africa, New Zealand, South America and even the rocks of frozen Antarctica.

How could all of these places, which are separated by thousands of kilometres of ocean today, have had the same plants living on them? These fossils gave geologists clues about one of the most important ideas of Earth science - that the continents move!

During the Permian Period, all the continents where Glossopteris had been found were huddled around the South Pole, as part of the ancient SUPERcontinent 'Pangaea'. The southern part of this supercontinent was covered in lush Glossopteris forests. But hang on a minute how could Glossopteris thrive at the South Pole, a frozen wasteland region today?
