Walking the deserts of mid-west USA or outback Australia, you may stub your toe on a fossil poking up out of the ground. A closer look may surprise you the types of fossils you find may not be what you expected in the desert!

In some deserts, the most common fossils you'll find will be of ocean-dwelling creatures, like clams, sea snails or even monstrous marine reptiles. But how did they end up there, so far from their watery homes?

The climate of the Earth is constantly changing: when the world warms, the ice sitting at the Earth's poles melts. The water from that melting ice has to go somewhere, and it pours into the world's oceans, raising the sea level by up to 200 m higher than today! Major sea level fluctuations involve up to 500 m rise, but this results from tectonics, not ice melting. With sea levels so high, the low-lying areas of the continents, including many deserts, become covered with seawater!
