Water Miners

To obtain water, desert plants have some special adaptations. Very deep roots can access water that has seeped far below the ground surface. In some cases, these roots bring water up into the soil, and from there, shallow roots use it during the day. This process is called 'hydraulic lift'.

On the other hand, many desert shrubs have shallow roots that extend outwards from the stem in all directions. Using these spreading roots, the plant gathers water from quite a large area in its surroundings. Because of this, desert plants often space themselves apart quite widely, so that each can have a share of the rain that soaks into the soil. A special form of plant spacing is called 'tiger bush'. This is where plants grow in groves or thickets, separated by bare areas that have no plants.
