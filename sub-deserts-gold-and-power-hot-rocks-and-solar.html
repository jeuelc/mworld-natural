Hot Rocks and Solar Power

The interior of the Earth is very hot more than 3000°C. Usually, heat escapes steadily through the ground. But where the land is blanketed by thick sands or sediments, the heat can be trapped. Under large areas of the inland deserts of Australia, the rocks a few kilometres below the ground are sometimes over 200°C. Water pumped down into these rocks can generate steam. In turn, the steam can be used to drive generators for electricity. This 'geothermal energy' is abundant, and we will be able to rely on it long after oil and gas become scarce.

Instead of large farms producing food, some deserts now have large 'farms' containing thousands of solar panels that absorb sunlight and convert it into electricity. One problem is that grit from desert dust storms can settle on the solar panels, reducing their efficiency. The panels then need regular washing to keep them generating power efficiently.

Both solar and geothermal power are much better for the atmosphere than burning fossil fuels.
