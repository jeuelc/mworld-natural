Buffering and Connecting Parks

 
click here click here

Sometimes parks are surrounded by 'buffer zones'. These are strips of land used to minimise the chance of stuff you don't want getting into the park invasive species, pollution, that kind of thing. It's like putting a protective bubble around the park, to make sure it isn't harmed.

You can also preserve strips of natural habitat to connect parks so that species can move between them. These are called 'ecological habitat corridors'. It is best to consider parks as a network of protected areas rather than as isolated patches of habitat. This is because many species respond to things that stress them by moving to new areas.

Figuring out everything about parks is called 'conservation planning'. Parks need to be in the best spots for their protection. Being close to cities or surrounded by agriculture or industry can all have different 'edge effects' on the health of our parks.

Edge effects aren't just pollution. They can be something as simple as a pet rabbit escaping from your backyard and making its way into a park!
